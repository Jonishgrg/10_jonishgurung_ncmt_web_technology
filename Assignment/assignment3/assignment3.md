## 1. Differentiate between Legibility and Readability

Legibility is basically how easy it is to see the letters on a page or a screen. For example, if you look at a font like Arial or Times New Roman, each letter is very clear and easy to tell apart from the others, even if the size is small. But if you use something really fancy like a cursive or decorative font, it might look nice but becomes hard to read because some letters can look similar, like “l” and “i” or “O” and “0”. Things like spacing between letters, the thickness of the strokes, and the overall design of the font affect legibility a lot. Readability is more about how easy it is to read the text as a whole, like a paragraph or an article. Even if the letters are very clear, if the sentences are too long, paragraphs are too crowded, or the spacing between lines is too small, reading becomes tiring. For example, a website might use a very clean font but cram all the text together on a bright background, making it hard for people to read without straining. In real life, designers have to consider both. You want the letters to be clear and recognizable, but you also want the overall text to be easy and pleasant to read. It’s like legibility is about recognizing letters, and readability is about understanding what those letters form when they are combined. When both are good, people can read quickly and comfortably, which is important for websites, books, and any kind of written content.

## 2. What is the “Rule of Thirds” in layout design?

The Rule of Thirds is a really simple but very effective way to make things look balanced and interesting. You imagine your page or image is divided into a grid of nine equal parts using two horizontal and two vertical lines. Instead of putting everything in the center, you place important elements along the lines or at the points where the lines cross. For example, if you are designing a website header, you could put the logo on the top-left intersection and a call-to-action button somewhere on the bottom-right intersection. This looks much better than just slapping everything in the middle because it creates natural balance and guides the viewer’s eyes across the page. Photographers use this a lot too — instead of putting the subject in the center, they place it off-center, which feels more dynamic and professional. On a website, it can make the design feel more organized, more alive, and less boring. Even if a person doesn’t consciously notice it, their eyes naturally move along the lines and intersections, which helps them notice the most important parts of the page first.

## 3. Why should you limit the number of font families used on a website?

Using too many different fonts on a website can make it look messy and unprofessional. If you use five or six different fonts, it becomes hard for the user to know where to look, and it distracts from the content. Limiting yourself to two or three fonts keeps the design clean and consistent. One font can be used for headings, another for body text, and maybe one small accent font for buttons or special highlights. Another reason is performance — every font you add increases the amount of data the browser has to load, which can slow down your website. Slower websites frustrate users, and many people leave before the page even fully loads. Also, too many fonts can ruin the visual hierarchy, which is how the reader notices which parts are more important. By sticking to a small number of fonts, you make the content easier to follow and more visually appealing. For example, if a tech website uses Roboto for headings and Open Sans for body text, it looks modern and neat. If they suddenly throw in Comic Sans, Impact, and Brush Script, it feels chaotic and distracting.

## 4. Define “Responsive Web Design”

Responsive web design is a way of building websites so that they work and look good on every kind of device. That means whether someone is using a big desktop monitor, a laptop, a tablet, or a small smartphone, the site adapts automatically. The layout changes, images resize, menus may collapse, and everything adjusts so the user doesn’t have to zoom or scroll sideways. For example, a website with three columns on a desktop might switch to one long vertical column on a phone so that the text is readable and buttons are easy to tap. The idea is to make the experience smooth and comfortable no matter what device people are using. This is really important today because most users visit websites on their phones. If a website is not responsive, it can be frustrating and drive people away. Responsive design uses things like fluid grids, flexible images, and CSS media queries to achieve this. It’s basically designing the site once but making sure it works everywhere automatically.

## 5. Explain the “F-Pattern” of scanning and how it influences content placement

When people visit websites, especially ones with lots of text like blogs, news sites, or article pages, they rarely read every single word from start to finish. Instead, they tend to scan in a certain pattern that researchers call the “F-Pattern.” Imagine the shape of the letter F. People first look across the top of the page, reading horizontally — this is usually where the headline or main navigation is. Then, they move down a bit and read across again, but this line is usually shorter than the first. Finally, their eyes travel down the left side of the page, scanning vertically along the beginnings of lines. This scanning behavior shows that the top and left side of a page get the most attention, while the bottom and right areas are looked at less.

This has huge implications for web design. Designers can use the F-pattern to strategically place the most important content where it’s more likely to be seen. For example, a company might place its logo or brand name in the top-left intersection, a call-to-action button along the top horizontal line, and a short summary or key points along the left vertical line. Less critical information, like advertisements, footnotes, or secondary links, can be placed on the right side or further down because users may not notice it. Understanding this pattern helps guide the user’s attention naturally, increasing the chances they will read or interact with key content. Even small design choices like aligning bullet points or headings to the left can support the F-pattern. In short, this pattern is a way to design websites that work with the natural behavior of readers, rather than against it.

## 6. Discuss the psychological impact of colors in web design. Examples of Blue, Red, and Green.

Colors aren’t just decoration; they influence emotions, thoughts, and decisions. Choosing the right colors in web design can change how a person feels about a brand or even what actions they take. Blue, for example, is widely used because it makes people feel calm, safe, and trustworthy. Banks, tech companies, and professional services often use blue because customers need to feel confident when entering personal information or making decisions. PayPal, LinkedIn, and Facebook all use blue for this reason — it gives a sense of reliability without being overwhelming.

Red is at the opposite end of the emotional spectrum. It grabs attention instantly, creates urgency, and can make people feel energetic or excited. This is why red is often used for sales banners, warning messages, or buttons where immediate action is desired. Think of Netflix or CNN — red is used for notifications or to highlight key content. Red can even increase heart rate and subconsciously make people act faster, which is perfect for calls to action.

Green, on the other hand, represents nature, growth, health, and calmness. It’s easy on the eyes and gives a sense of balance. Green is commonly used by brands related to health, eco-friendliness, or finance. Starbucks and Whole Foods use green to emphasize freshness, sustainability, and wellness. A website with too much green can feel bland, so it’s often combined with white or soft neutrals to maintain readability.

When choosing colors, designers also need to think about contrast, cultural associations, and user comfort. For instance, a website for a children’s health service might use bright green for energy and growth, while a law firm might use navy blue to convey trust and seriousness. Color psychology is about subtly guiding user emotions and decisions through visual cues, creating both an aesthetic appeal and a strategic advantage.

## 7. Explain the “60-30-10 Rule” for color balance

The 60-30-10 rule is a simple but extremely practical guideline for balancing colors on a website. The idea is to break the color scheme into three parts so that the website looks visually appealing and doesn’t overwhelm the user.

The first 60% is the main color, which usually covers backgrounds and large areas. This is typically a neutral color like white, light gray, beige, or a very soft muted tone. Its job is to provide a calm backdrop so that text and other elements can stand out. A good main color ensures readability, reduces eye strain, and allows the secondary and accent colors to be noticed.

The next 30% is the secondary color, used for headings, sidebars, menus, and other important sections. This color contrasts with the main color but doesn’t dominate the page. Its role is to organize content visually, guide the user’s eyes, and provide structure. For example, on a blog, the sidebar or category headers might use the secondary color so they are distinct from the main text but not distracting.

Finally, the remaining 10% is the accent color. This is a bright, bold color used sparingly on elements like call-to-action buttons, icons, or important links. Its purpose is to draw attention and encourage action. Overusing the accent color would reduce its impact, so it should be applied selectively. Following this 60-30-10 rule ensures a harmonious color palette, improves user experience, and makes a website look professional and well-thought-out. Designers can even test different combinations by creating mockups and adjusting percentages until the site feels visually balanced.

## 8. Slow website due to raw high-res images. Explain Image Optimization & file formats.

Images are one of the biggest factors affecting website speed. High-resolution raw images from cameras can easily be 10–20 MB or more. If such images are uploaded directly to a website, the pages will load very slowly, frustrating users and causing many to leave before the site even appears. This also affects search engine ranking, because Google favors faster-loading websites.

Image optimization is the process of reducing file size while maintaining visual quality. There are a few ways to do this. One is resizing images to the exact dimensions needed on the website rather than using the full camera resolution. Another is compressing images — JPEG files are great for photographs because they use lossy compression, which removes some data that the eye can’t notice, drastically reducing file size. PNG is better for graphics, icons, and images with flat colors or transparency because it preserves sharp edges, although files are usually larger.

Optimizing images improves user experience, makes websites load faster, and even helps reduce hosting costs. For example, a raw 15 MB image can often be compressed to 300–400 KB without noticeable quality loss, which dramatically reduces page load time. Proper image optimization also helps responsive design because smaller files adapt better on mobile devices where bandwidth may be limited. Overall, optimizing images is not just technical — it’s essential for keeping visitors happy, engaged, and coming back to the site.

## 10. Website looks fine on laptop but unreadable on phone. Missing principle & fix.

The main problem here is the website is not responsive. Without responsiveness, a website designed for desktop screens becomes difficult to use on mobile devices. Users have to zoom, scroll sideways, or pinch the screen to read content, which is frustrating and leads to higher bounce rates.

Responsive design fixes this by making sure layouts adapt to any screen size. One common tool is media queries in CSS. Media queries allow the site to apply different styles based on screen width or device type. For example, a three-column desktop layout might collapse into a single column on a mobile phone, making text readable and buttons easier to tap. Another key element is fluid grids, where elements use percentages instead of fixed pixel widths, so they scale automatically depending on the screen. Images can also be made flexible using relative widths so they resize without breaking the layout.

By combining media queries and fluid grids, a website can provide a consistent user experience across devices. This is especially important today, as most users access websites on their phones. A non-responsive site not only frustrates users but can also hurt search engine rankings because Google prioritizes mobile-friendly websites. The goal of responsive design is to create a seamless experience — users should be able to navigate, read, and interact with content easily whether they’re on a laptop, tablet, or smartphone.
