## 1. Differentiate between Legibility and Readability

Legibility is basically how easy it is to see the letters on a page or a screen. For example, if you look at a font like Arial or Times New Roman, each letter is very clear and easy to tell apart from the others, even if the size is small. But if you use something really fancy like a cursive or decorative font, it might look nice but becomes hard to read because some letters can look similar, like “l” and “i” or “O” and “0”. Things like spacing between letters, the thickness of the strokes, and the overall design of the font affect legibility a lot. Readability is more about how easy it is to read the text as a whole, like a paragraph or an article. Even if the letters are very clear, if the sentences are too long, paragraphs are too crowded, or the spacing between lines is too small, reading becomes tiring. For example, a website might use a very clean font but cram all the text together on a bright background, making it hard for people to read without straining. In real life, designers have to consider both. You want the letters to be clear and recognizable, but you also want the overall text to be easy and pleasant to read. It’s like legibility is about recognizing letters, and readability is about understanding what those letters form when they are combined. When both are good, people can read quickly and comfortably, which is important for websites, books, and any kind of written content.

## 2. What is the “Rule of Thirds” in layout design?

The Rule of Thirds is a really simple but very effective way to make things look balanced and interesting. You imagine your page or image is divided into a grid of nine equal parts using two horizontal and two vertical lines. Instead of putting everything in the center, you place important elements along the lines or at the points where the lines cross. For example, if you are designing a website header, you could put the logo on the top-left intersection and a call-to-action button somewhere on the bottom-right intersection. This looks much better than just slapping everything in the middle because it creates natural balance and guides the viewer’s eyes across the page. Photographers use this a lot too — instead of putting the subject in the center, they place it off-center, which feels more dynamic and professional. On a website, it can make the design feel more organized, more alive, and less boring. Even if a person doesn’t consciously notice it, their eyes naturally move along the lines and intersections, which helps them notice the most important parts of the page first.

## 3. Why should you limit the number of font families used on a website?

Using too many different fonts on a website can make it look messy and unprofessional. If you use five or six different fonts, it becomes hard for the user to know where to look, and it distracts from the content. Limiting yourself to two or three fonts keeps the design clean and consistent. One font can be used for headings, another for body text, and maybe one small accent font for buttons or special highlights. Another reason is performance — every font you add increases the amount of data the browser has to load, which can slow down your website. Slower websites frustrate users, and many people leave before the page even fully loads. Also, too many fonts can ruin the visual hierarchy, which is how the reader notices which parts are more important. By sticking to a small number of fonts, you make the content easier to follow and more visually appealing. For example, if a tech website uses Roboto for headings and Open Sans for body text, it looks modern and neat. If they suddenly throw in Comic Sans, Impact, and Brush Script, it feels chaotic and distracting.

## 4. Define “Responsive Web Design”

Responsive web design is a way of building websites so that they work and look good on every kind of device. That means whether someone is using a big desktop monitor, a laptop, a tablet, or a small smartphone, the site adapts automatically. The layout changes, images resize, menus may collapse, and everything adjusts so the user doesn’t have to zoom or scroll sideways. For example, a website with three columns on a desktop might switch to one long vertical column on a phone so that the text is readable and buttons are easy to tap. The idea is to make the experience smooth and comfortable no matter what device people are using. This is really important today because most users visit websites on their phones. If a website is not responsive, it can be frustrating and drive people away. Responsive design uses things like fluid grids, flexible images, and CSS media queries to achieve this. It’s basically designing the site once but making sure it works everywhere automatically.

## 5. Explain the “F-Pattern” of scanning and how it influences content placement

When people read websites with a lot of text, like news articles, blogs, or documentation pages, they don’t read every single word. Studies using eye-tracking technology have shown that they tend to scan in an “F” shape. First, they read across the top horizontally, usually looking at the header or main title. Then, they move a little lower and read across a second line, which is usually shorter than the first. After that, they scan down the left side of the page vertically, mostly just looking at the beginnings of lines. When you connect these movements, it forms something like the letter F. This pattern is really important for web design because it shows where the user’s attention naturally goes. Designers use this information to place the most important elements — like logos, headings, navigation menus, or buttons — in these hot zones where people are most likely to see them. Less important content, like secondary links, advertisements, or long explanations, can go further down the page or to the right, because users are less likely to notice it. Understanding the F-pattern can make the difference between a website that feels intuitive and easy to navigate and one where users get lost or miss important information. For example, if you place a signup button on the top left or along the first line of text, it will get noticed faster than putting it somewhere in the bottom right corner.

## 6. Discuss the psychological impact of colors in web design. Examples of Blue, Red, and Green.

Colors affect how people feel and even how they behave. They can create emotions and influence decisions, which is why they are so important in web design. Blue, for example, is associated with trust, reliability, and calmness. That’s why banks, tech companies, and corporate websites often use blue — think PayPal or LinkedIn. Blue makes users feel safe and confident, which is crucial when they are entering sensitive information or making decisions. Red, on the other hand, is high-energy and attention-grabbing. It can create excitement, urgency, and even increase heart rate. That’s why red is used for sales, alerts, or buttons that you want people to click immediately — like Netflix or CNN. Green represents health, growth, and nature. It’s soothing to the eyes and conveys balance. Brands that focus on wellness, eco-friendly products, or money often use green — like Starbucks or Whole Foods. Choosing the right color for a website isn’t just about aesthetics; it’s about creating a feeling and guiding user behavior. Colors can help build trust, draw attention, create excitement, or make users feel relaxed, depending on what you want them to do on the site.

## 7. Explain the “60-30-10 Rule” for color balance

The 60-30-10 rule is a simple guideline for using colors effectively on a website so it doesn’t look chaotic or boring. The first part, 60%, is the main color — usually a neutral like white, gray, or beige — which covers the majority of the site, including backgrounds and large sections. This creates a calm canvas for the other elements. The second part, 30%, is the secondary or supporting color, which is used for headings, sidebars, and other important sections. It contrasts with the main color but isn’t too distracting. Finally, the last 10% is the accent color, which is bright or bold and used sparingly on buttons, icons, or links. This draws attention to the most important actions or information. Following this rule creates a balanced, professional-looking website that guides the user’s eyes naturally to the key points without overwhelming them with too many colors.

## 8. Slow website due to raw high-res images. Explain Image Optimization & file formats.

High-resolution raw images from cameras are usually very large, often tens of megabytes. If you put them directly on a website, it will load very slowly, which annoys users and can hurt search engine ranking. Image optimization means reducing the file size of an image without losing too much quality. For photographs, JPEG is the best choice because it uses lossy compression — some data is removed to make the file smaller, but visually it still looks good. PNG is better for logos, icons, or images that need transparency, because it keeps all the details but files are bigger. Designers often resize images to fit the website layout, compress them to reduce size, and choose the right format. Optimized images make websites load faster, improve user experience, and even help SEO because search engines prefer faster sites.

## 9. Law Firm wants bright yellow background + Comic Sans. Critique & suggest alternatives.

Using a bright yellow background with Comic Sans for a law firm website is a bad idea. Yellow is very bright and can make text hard to read for a long time, and Comic Sans looks casual and even childish, which is the opposite of professional. For a law firm, the goal is to look trustworthy, professional, and serious. Better options would be serif fonts like Times New Roman or Garamond, which give a traditional, reliable feel. Sans-serif fonts like Helvetica or Open Sans can also work if you want a modern, clean look. Colors should be more subdued, like navy blue, dark gray, or even black, to convey stability and professionalism. Accent colors like gold or white can be used sparingly to make the design elegant. This combination will make the firm look credible, serious, and approachable without hurting readability.

## 10. Website looks fine on laptop but unreadable on phone. Missing principle & fix.

The problem here is that the website is not responsive. On mobile, people shouldn’t have to zoom or scroll sideways. Responsive design fixes this by making layouts adapt to different screen sizes. One way to do this is using CSS media queries, which apply specific rules depending on screen width. For example, a menu that appears horizontally on desktop can stack vertically on a phone. Another way is using fluid grids, which use percentages instead of fixed pixels, so elements automatically scale to fit the screen. Together, media queries and fluid grids make sure the website looks good and works well on any device. Responsive design is essential today because most users access websites on phones or tablets, and a site that isn’t mobile-friendly can lose visitors very quickly.
